Level 3 - Rank B - Lesson 2: Finding problems
See

Biz Tip

The first step of problem solving is to find problems.

Biz Tip

Using trigger questions can help you identify recurring issues and anticipate future problems as well.

Example

Jerry:

OK, so the first step is called problem-finding. As the name suggests, it means you need to actively look for problems to fix.

Tomo:

Right. So, you mean to improve things?

Jerry:

Yes, but I want you to change that mindset. If something is not performing at its best, then it’s a problem. And it’s your job to find out what that is and to fix it.

Tomo:

OK, OK. So how do I find these problems if they’re not obvious?

Jerry:

It’s important to look at what you and your team are doing from different perspectives.

Tomo:

And how do I do that?

Jerry:

Well, you can ask yourself and your team trigger questions.

Tomo:

Trigger questions?

Jerry:

Yes, questions that can help you see things on a larger scale.

Tomo:

OK, so what kind of questions should I ask?

Jerry:

What would make your customers happier? What are some potential risks? What processes take time and effort? What are your competitors doing that you’re not doing?

Tomo:

I think I get what you mean. If it isn’t perfect, then we have a problem.

Try

Answer these questions.

  • How do you usually identify issues at work?
  • What work-related problems have you identified?
  • What trigger questions can you think of to identify the right problem to solve?
Act

Role play

Explain the first step of the problem-solving process and what trigger questions are. Give examples of trigger questions.
