Biz Tip

Problem-solving shouldn’t stop once the solution has been implemented. Strive to further improve quality, processes, productivity, etc.

Biz Tip

The problem-solving cycle goes hand in hand with the Japanese philosophy called “Kaizen” which means continuous improvement.

Example

Tomo:

OK, so we’re now down to the last stage of the process, right?

Jerry:

Well, I wouldn’t say it’s the final step of the process, Tomo.

Tomo:

What do you mean?

Jerry:

Implementing a solution doesn’t necessarily mean you’ve already solved the problem.

Tomo:

I see. Does that mean I need to monitor what happens next and spot any problems that may arise?

Jerry:

That’s right. You have to make sure that your solution works smoothly as planned.

Tomo:

So what should I do when new issues arise from the implemented solution?

Jerry:

You just have to repeat the problem-solving cycle. Identify the problem, gather the facts and so on, until you’ve implemented another solution.

Tomo:

I see. So it’s not different from Kaizen after all.

Jerry:

Exactly! In other words, strive for continuous improvement. Whether it’s quality, productivity or a process, there’s always room for small improvements.

Tomo:

It makes perfect sense now. That’s why problem-solving isn’t really a linear process but a cycle.

Jerry:

Right. So are you ready to sit down with your team and look for problems?

Tomo:

Yes, I am. Thanks for all your advice, Jerry.

Try

Answer these questions.

  • Has your company implemented something new lately?
  • What steps are being taken to ensure that the new process or system works smoothly?
  • What do you think can be done to further improve it?
Act

Role play

Imagine that a colleague is asking you for advice on problem-solving. Go over the entire problem-solving process.
