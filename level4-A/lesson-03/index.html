We all live in a world bound by rules and regulations. Some follow these rules to the letter whereas others bend or even break the rules depending on certain circumstances. This first dimension of culture, universalism versus particularism, defines how much flexibility a particular culture allows.
Universalism:
People from universalist cultures believe that rules need to be followed regardless of the situation. If the rules are broken, one must face the consequences — no exceptions. For example, in Germany, anyone trying to cross the street when the light is red, even if there are no cars, would be looked down upon.
Particularism:
People from particularist cultures believe that rules are general guidelines. Relationships and circumstances, more than laws and regulations, often dictate one’s behaviour. For example, in Russia, it would not be uncommon for a person to lie to the police to keep a friend from being arrested.
If people from these two cultures impose their values onto others without thought or consideration, problems are bound to occur. Preventing conflict is key, and Stephen R. Covey’s “The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People,” prescribes the first step: in order to be understood, one must first seek to understand.
