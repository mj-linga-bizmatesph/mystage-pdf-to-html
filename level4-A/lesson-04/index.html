All human beings have their own inclinations and interests. However, depending on one’s upbringing and education, personal and self-focused interests may either be encouraged or discouraged in favor of the larger interests of one’s community. Our second dimension of culture explores this in more detail.
Individualism:
In an individualistic culture, people are free to pursue their own interests, make their own decisions, and are recognized independently for personal achievements. The “American Dream” is the epitome of individualism, where people believe that hard work and freedom lead to success.
Communitarianism:
In a communitarian culture, the group comes first. The needs of the many outweigh the needs of the few, and sacrifices are expected to be made in exchange for protection, acceptance, and group prosperity. In Japan, the concept of lifetime employment is a prime example of communitarian values.
Though conflict between these two cultures seems unavoidable, establishing guidelines and objectives that both parties can refer to may be a good first step in building a strong, dependable relationship. After all, communication and understanding are the bedrock of any successful partnership.
