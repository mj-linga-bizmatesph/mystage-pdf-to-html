How do you view your boss outside of the office? Is he or she just another regular person that you happen to work with? Or do you show the same level of respect and deference as you would at work? Some do, some don’t. Let’s explore this more in the fourth dimension of culture: specific and diffuse.
Specific:
Specific-oriented cultures separate their professional and personal lives and believe that relationships have little bearing on achieving objectives. For instance, in the U.S., friendly relationships are quickly created, only for the relationship to abruptly end once the goal has been achieved.
Diffuse:
On the other hand, diffuse-oriented cultures treat relationships as all-encompassing. For them, business is personal and one’s professional relationship with others extends well into their private lives. For example, in Japan, much time and patience may be required for relationships to form; however, it is not uncommon for business partners to become lifelong friends.
Some may view specific-oriented people as frivolous time wasters, or diffuse-oriented people as cold and difficult to read. However, it’s important to note that business success also depends on relationships, among other factors. Taking the time to recognize each other’s priorities can help both parties strike a balance that they can benefit from.
