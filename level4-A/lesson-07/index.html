Status and success are common motivators that appear in many societies across the globe. However, where this status originates from differs from culture to culture. In our fifth dimension of culture, we will explore how status is achieved or ascribed across cultures.
Achievement:
In an achievement-oriented culture, one attains status through what they have accomplished. Therefore, one’s track record, awards, and unique achievements are highly valued. For example, in Norway, it isn’t uncommon to see bright, talented youngsters climb the ranks and surpass his or her elders.
Ascription:
In an ascription-oriented culture, one’s status is determined by who they are. This includes things like age, gender, family, university, or social connections — to name a few. For example, in the Middle East and North Africa, one’s suitability for any particular job may be determined by the candidate’s alma mater.
These two cultures may not see eye to eye on how status is determined, but their distinction reveals a common drive for excellence. Understanding differences sometimes leads one to discover important similar values between seemingly opposing cultures, allowing both parties to work towards mutual success.
