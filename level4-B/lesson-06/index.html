Picture this: someone asks you, “What do you do for a living?” What would you say? Do you begin with your company’s name or your job title? Although there is no wrong answer, the difference in how individuals identify themselves as employees establishes the fourth dimension of organizational culture.
Local:
Employees in local cultures see themselves as part of a team and focus on developing close relationships, sharing information, and camaraderie. However, due to the intimate nature of this culture, personal conflicts with colleagues and office politics can run rampant if left unchecked.
Professional:
Employees in professional cultures see themselves as specialists assigned to specific tasks. As work is often project-based, employees are able to focus on the work itself without worrying about politics and long-lasting relationships. However, issues may arise from the resulting lack of cohesion, frequent headhunting, and turnovers.
As businesses continue to expand overseas, many local companies are finding the need to become more professional in their approach. Working on multiple international projects with different people is becoming the norm — thus, the need to adopt different business styles becomes more apparent.
