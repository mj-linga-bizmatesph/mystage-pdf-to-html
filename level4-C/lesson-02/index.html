The first thing to consider when exploring occupational culture is the amount of know-how needed for a particular job. Certain occupations may require expert-level knowledge whereas others may require less. This ultimately affects everything, from group association to retention and identity.

Esoteric knowledge

Highly specialized knowledge often takes years of dedication, training, and patience to acquire. As a result, those who have persevered are likely to develop high levels of pride, become more involved with their work, and develop a strong sense of belonging to their particular occupation.

General knowledge

Occupations that require less specialized knowledge and a more generic understanding of basic business processes may result in a culture that is more process-oriented. This work style may lead to easier adjustment periods but a higher degree of turnover and interchangeability.
Regardless of the degree of proficiency one’s profession requires, it is also important to note that knowledge opens doors to opportunities. However, it is the effective application of that knowledge that enables us to turn opportunities into success.
