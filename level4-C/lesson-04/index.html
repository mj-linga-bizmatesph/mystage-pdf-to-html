SEE

Read the article and take note of the highlighted phrases.

In the office, it is not unusual for some to view those belonging to different vocations as outsiders. Despite being in the same company, unique occupational cultures emerge from the different responsibilities, mentalities, motivations, and levels of pride each employee has.

High Occupational Identification:

Those with high degrees of occupational identification are dedicated, loyal, and emotionally attached to their work. Good as this may be, an excessive amount of pride due to high identification may also result in adversity and suspicion towards other occupations, causing internal rifts and conflict.

Low Occupational Identification:

Employees with low levels of occupational identification are driven by simpler motivations when compared to those with high occupational identity. Although they might not have the highest amount of attachment to their work, they are typically classified as rational, pragmatic, and down-to-earth.
In any organization, divisions are made apparent by its employees’ differing levels of occupational identification. Nonetheless, it is important to keep in mind the big picture: that no matter how superior one’s profession may seem when compared to another, a company achieves more when its employees work together.
