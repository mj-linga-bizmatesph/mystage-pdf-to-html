Now that we have an understanding of the four main generations, let us examine the balance and distribution of these groups and how the global workforce is expected to change in the coming years.
As of 2016, millennials have become the largest generational population in America. As a result, the last 50 years of boomer-defined workplace cultures are shifting rapidly to accommodate 21 st century values. By 2025, millennials are projected to make up 75% of the entire American workforce, forcing many to re-examine their approach to business, leadership, and cross-generational collaboration.
As boomers continue to retire from the workplace at a rate of nearly 10,000 per day, traditional notions of rank and age are also shrinking. This leaves many Gen Xers in difficult positions: ones where age and experience no longer clearly define roles or command respect. According to research, nearly one in three report to someone younger, and one in seven report to bosses a full decade younger. This has made working with — or in some cases, under — millennials the biggest challenge for Gen Xers.
Now, with iGeners entering the American workforce at a rate of 4.5 million per year, a paradigm shift in workplace culture and cross-generational collaboration is upon the business world. Without a solid understanding of the generational landscape, conflict and instability are sure to run rampant.
