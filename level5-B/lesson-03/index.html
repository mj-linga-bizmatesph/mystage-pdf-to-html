Back in the late 70’s, if you walked into any large enterprise, you would probably come across the colossal mainframe computers that powered the company’s database. Only a handful of programming wizards knew how to use them – and that was just to manage data input and output. Then came Lawrence Ellison, a university dropout who worked as a programmer in California for eight years. Together with his former boss, he set up what would later become Oracle Systems Corporation.
Their company was off to a rough start but stumbled upon an IBM research paper describing a new programming language called SQL. Unlike his competitors, Ellison seized the opportunity and rewrote the language so it could be run on any computer. From then on, Oracle’s business skyrocketed. But for all of Ellison’s ferociousness, Oracle began to make promises it could not keep. Many times, Oracle falsely claimed compatibility with several platforms and hardware.
After some reflection, Ellison realized that it was his mess to clean. Oracle needed to start over. He created a new management team and fired everyone else. They liquidated accounts and tightened financial control. A new system was put into place that rewarded sales reps only when a product was shipped. Oracle then became the gold standard for database technology and applications for enterprises worldwide – serving the largest multinational company to the smallest coffee shop.
