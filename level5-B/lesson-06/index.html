Read the article.

Fire-eating, accordion-playing and stilt-walking: these are not the usual skills and job experience you’d expect to find in a CEO’s resume but Guy Laliberté, CEO of one of Canada’s largest cultural exports, Cirque du Soleil, did all of these and more. Once a street performer, he won a grant from the Quebec government to put on a unique show to celebrate the 450th anniversary of Canada’s discovery. Since then, Laliberté has brought Cirque to a global scale.
 Cirque du Soleil’s success, however, happened at a time when the circus business had been in a long-term decline. Other forms of entertainment and an increase in animal rights awareness made the circus business a hard sell. However, Laliberté’s strategy was not to compete within the current circus industry but to create its own space. While the traditional circus catered to children, Cirque pulled in a new group of consumers – adults and corporate clients who were fans of opera and ballet.
This new audience was willing to spend, as long as they were treated to a unique experience. Cirque owes its success to Guy Laliberté’s strategic thinking. He knew the current situation of the industry – declining audience share and big competitors – and decided that rather than trying his luck against these odds, he would make his own. Through this strategy, he was able to create his own audience and bring Cirque du Soleil to the triumph and adoration that it enjoys today.
