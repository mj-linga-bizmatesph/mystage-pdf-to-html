Cassius Marcellus Clay, Jr. or more popularly known as Muhammad Ali, grew up in a time of racial discrimination. Some suggest that this made him passionate for boxing. With extraordinary talent and commitment, he began training when he was 12. He had his first amateur bout and win in 1954. In 1960, he became famous for winning an Olympic Gold Medal and more so in 1964, when he knocked out unbeatable Sonny Liston, becoming the heavyweight champion at the age of 22.
Despite all of this, Muhammad Ali was stripped of all his titles and was forced into a hiatus in 1967 after he refused to be drafted into the Vietnam War, but he had a passionate comeback in 1970, when he fought in what was called the “Fight of the Century” against Joe Frazier, who gave Ali his first defeat as a pro. But, true to his calling as a champion, he beat Frazier six months later and continued to dominate the boxing world as he persevered and punched his way to victories.
Muhammad Ali called himsef “The Greatest.” His conquests came from intense training and dedication. He was the first to come into the gym and the last to leave. At first, he ran from his apartment because he didn't have money for transportation. He didn't settle for mediocrity; he knew he was meant to be a champ. Ali believed that “Impossible is not a fact. It’s an opinion. Impossible is not a declaration. It’s a dare. Impossible is potential. Impossible is temporary. Impossible is nothing.”
