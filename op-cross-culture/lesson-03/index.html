DEFINING YOUR OWN
CULTURE FIRST
3
BIZ TIP
Before you even begin to learn about other cultures, have a good understanding of your own. You can do this by:
Knowing what’s typical of your own culture
Knowing how your views compare to other cultures
Thinking about why your views are different from others
SEE
Ron: So Ken, you must be excited about your one-year assignment overseas.
Ken: Oh yes. I took your advice and now I’m reading about the country’s culture and customs.
Ron: Good for you. I recommend that you study your own culture, too.
Ken: But I already know my culture. I live it every day.
Ron: Of course you do, but we all live in our own culture without really thinking about it, and this prevents us from fully understanding our own culture.
Ken: Really? But I’m aware of our traditions, customs, and celebrations.
Ron: I don’t mean things like that. I mean things like how your culture views rules and laws, whether personal achievement is more or less important than team success in your culture, or how openly people in your culture express emotions, and so on.
Ken: Oh, I’ve never really thought of things like that.
▼▼▼
TRY

Answer these questions and compare your answer with your trainer’s. Discuss the differences and similarities. (1 = not at all, 2 = a little bit, 3 = quite a bit, 4 = very much so)

1 2 3 4

  1. Is it important to follow the law and rules in your culture? (jaywalking, taking bribes, avoiding taxes, etc.)

  1. Are individual needs more important than your group’s needs in your culture? (individual performance vs team performance, your needs vs family needs, loyalty to your company, etc.)

  1. Are you free to express your emotions in front of others? (strongly express your opinions in meetings, raise your voice, banging on the table at work, etc.)

  1. Are one’s personal life and business life closely linked in your culture? (your boss is your boss outside of work, your seniors will always be your seniors, etc.)

  1. Is background an important factor in your culture? (age, gender, family background, education, title, company name, etc.)

  1. Is time very important in your culture? (meeting deadlines, starting and finishing meetings on time, etc.)

  1. Do people believe that they have complete control of your destiny in your culture? (you are accountable for everything that happens to you)

▼▼▼
ACT

Explain what you’ve learned about your culture and how it might differ from others to a junior colleague.
