Giving advice on e-mail writing
9
BIZ TIP
A good e-mail is made up of good structure, good content, and good mechanics. Before you send an e-mail, ask yourself three questions.
  • Does it have a clear purpose?
  • Is the content clear?
  • Is it reader-oriented?
SEE

Example

Dave:
Hi Taro. How do you feel about writing English e-mails now?
Taro:
Much more confident now, thanks to you. I’ve learned so much about e-mail writing.
Dave:
Great. What have you learned?
Taro:
Well, I’ve learned that an e-mail should have proper structure. The subject line needs to be clear. The greeting and closing should depend on who the reader is. The opening and closing paragraphs create the tone of the e-mail. And of course, I should have a proper signature block.
Dave:
Great. What about the content itself?
Taro:
Yes, that’s important too. I should keep it positive, short, and simple.
Dave:
What about the difference between formal and informal e-mails?
Taro
Right. I used to think that business e-mails needed to always be formal. However, now I know that the formality really depends on who the reader is. Last but not least, I should take note of using proper mechanics.
Dave:
And don’t forget the three questions that you can ask yourself before sending your e-mail.
Taro
I’ve got them memorized, Dave. Does it have a clear purpose? Is the content clear? Is it reader-oriented?
Dave:
Exactly. And if you can’t say yes to these questions, don’t send that e-mail.

TRY

Answer the questions below.

  1. What part of the e-mail structure is the most difficult for you to write? (the subject line, the greeting and closing, the opening and closing paragraphs, etc.)
  2. What are some things that you can do to keep your e-mail positive?
  3. What is the KISS rule?
  4. What are some examples of formal and informal e-mail expressions?
  5. How can you add a personal touch to an e-mail?
  6. What are some things that you need to take note of when reviewing the mechanics of an e-mail?

ACT

Role play

Part 1:
Give advice to a colleague on all the things that he or she can do to write better e-mails in English.

Part 2:
For further practice, write a work-related e-mail. Do it for homework if you run out of time.
