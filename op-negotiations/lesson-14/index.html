LEARNING FROM THE OTHER SIDE
14
BIZ TIP
Many people confuse “understanding the other side” with “learning from the other side.” In negotiations, we need to learn the real reasons behind what the other person says. Learn from the other side by:
Asking open-ended questions to get a general understanding
Pressing for details to understand the specifics
Asking questions with the purpose to learn from the other side
SEE
Ken is speaking to his mentor, Ron, about starting his negotiation.
Ken I plan to ask my counterpart questions to understand their needs.
Ron That’s a good approach, but be sure to learn from him and not only understand him.
Ken I’m confident in my active listening skills because I used to work in customer service.
Ron You can use active listening with an angry customer but not with your counterpart in a negotiation. Your goal is not just to understand them, but to learn from them.
Ken I don’t understand the difference.
Ron OK, let me ask you about your lunch. So, can you tell me what you had for lunch today?
Ken Sure. I had pasta.
Ron Did you have any vegetables?
Ken There was some broccoli in the pasta.
Ron Is there a reason you didn’t order salad?
Ken Well, I just wanted a quick lunch.
Ron So, if you had had more time, would you have ordered a salad?
Ken Well,...
Ron You see. I started with an Open-Ended question, “Can you tell me…?” And then, I pressed for details with a Closed question, “Did you have any…?” And I asked questions with the purpose to learn, such as “Is there a reason…?” and “So, if you had more time,…?”
Ken I think I get it. By doing this, you can really learn from the other side.
