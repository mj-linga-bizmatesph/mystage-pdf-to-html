BIZ TIP
The words you emphasize can totally change the meaning of a simple sentence.
a: I didn’t say my TV was broken. (Not my TV, Tom’s TV)
b: I didn’t say my TV was broken. (Not my TV, my telephone.)
SEE

Do you know these words and phrases related to television?

  • His couch potato days are over. I’m glad he took up a new hobby.
  • A lot of viewers tuned in to listen to the President’s announcement.
  • I think the clicker’s broken. I can’t turn up the volume.
  • Their ratings went up when they aired the pilot episode last night.
▼▼▼
TRY

Let’s talk about television. Let the trainer take the lead in asking you questions. Here are some questions to get you started.

  • What foreign TV programs are popular in our country?
  • What sort of TV shows are popular among men in your country? Women?
  • Do you think there’s too much violence on TV?
  • Is censorship strict on TV in your country?

Let’s switch roles. Now you take the lead in asking questions and giving your opinion. Here are some questions to get you started.

  • Do you think you can survive a month without TV?
  • Does your family spend time together watching television?
  • What do you usually watch?
  • What sort of TV shows do you find least appealing to watch?
